# About pyChing and PancakeBunny

## The Origin Story

In 1999, Stephen M. Gava created pyChing - a Python program for casting and interpreting I Ching hexagrams. It was elegant, functional, and faithful to the ancient tradition.

By 2006, it stopped being maintained. By the 2020s, it no longer worked on modern systems.

## Enter PancakeBunny

PancakeBunny is a persona inspired by [Oolong the rabbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oolong_(rabbit)), an early internet meme from 1999-2003. Oolong could balance things on his head - most famously what appeared to be a pancake (actually a dorayaki, a Japanese confection).

The meme's caption: "I have no idea what I'm talking about, so here's a picture of a bunny with a pancake on its head."

This became shorthand for approaching the unknown with honesty and humor rather than false expertise.

## Guiding Principles

Based on `project_notes.txt`, which PancakeBunny wrote:

### Cultural Reverence
Preserve the authentic I Ching oracle methodology and traditions. This is a 3000-year-old wisdom tradition deserving of respect.

### Honor the Craft
Maintain and acknowledge Stephen M. Gava's architecture and all contributors. This software has history and that matters.

### Universal Access
Make pyChing accessible across platforms, languages, abilities, and technical contexts. Don't gatekeep wisdom.

## "What Would PancakeBunny Do?"

When facing a decision in this project, ask:

1. **Humility Check**: Am I approaching this with appropriate uncertainty?

## For Contributors

If you want to contribute to pyChing:

1. **Respect the tradition** - The I Ching isn't a toy or a game
2. **Keep PancakeBunny's spirit** - Approach with humility
3. **Test everything** - Especially oracle logic
4. **Document clearly** - Help others understand
5. **Stay focused** - Simple is better than complex
